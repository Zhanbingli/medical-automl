What This Project Studies

The core question is simple: when a clinical prediction pipeline leaves its training distribution, what kind of failure appears first, and how much of that failure comes from model class versus representation design?

  • Internal data: UCI Heart Disease (`n=303`), 5-fold stratified cross-validation.
  • External data: Kaggle Heart Failure Prediction (`n=918`), aligned to the UCI schema.
  • Compared systems: custom tabular-to-text Transformer, Qwen3.5-2B under 0/1/3/5/10-shot prompting, and eight tabular baselines.
  • Primary outcome: external reliability under schema mismatch and missing-value ambiguity.

Important Constraint

This is a case study, not a perfectly controlled architecture bake-off. The external cohort does not natively contain the UCI `ca` and `thal` variables, the Transformer consumes a Chinese serialized template, and the LLM consumes an English narrative with explicit missing-value wording.

Main Findings

  • The custom Transformer performs reasonably internally, then loses specificity on the aligned external cohort.
  • The LLM does not look stable at zero-shot; its main issue is calibration, not immediate specificity preservation.
  • Five-shot prompting gives the strongest external neural profile in this repo.
  • Attention comparisons suggest that the aligned `thal=0` token disrupts routing, but this remains probe-level evidence.
  • Ablation results suggest tokenization familiarity can matter more than statistically conventional imputation.

Why This Matters

The practical lesson is not “use one model family everywhere.” It is that external reliability in clinical AI is shaped by representation details: schema alignment, missing-value wording, serialization language, and how much distributional structure a pipeline hard-codes during training or in context.
